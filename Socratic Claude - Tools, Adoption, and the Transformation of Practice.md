# Socratic Claude - Tools, Adoption, and the Transformation of Practice

Jaime: I was thinking that, since we can use LLMs as tools to flesh out particular ideas (i.e. I can say: develop a skill that does *this* the way I do it), we might be entering an age of much greater specificity in how we describe what we do. Maybe some of those things will even be called by the names we give these tools.

Claude: That touches on something worth sitting with. You're suggesting that the act of making something explicit—codifying how you work into a tool—might actually reshape how we speak about it, even how we understand it.

But I wonder: when we name a practice after its tool, does the tool become just a vehicle for something that already existed? Or does the formalization itself change the nature of what we're doing?

Put differently—if someone uses your skill, are they doing what you do, or are they doing *something the skill enables*?

Jaime: That's an interesting thought. I'd say it might be that, even if it existed before, it wasn't widely adopted. And that adoption reshapes it.

Claude: That's the crux of it, isn't it. There's a difference between something existing and something becoming *shared practice*. When a way of working moves from solitary intuition to codified, reproducible form—available to many—it ceases to be the same thing, even if the mechanics are identical.

The skill doesn't just distribute what you do. It standardizes it, makes it teachable, scales it. And in doing so, it becomes something new in the world.

So when you said practices might be called by the names of the tools—I think you're pointing to something deeper than just language. You're suggesting the tool is what makes the practice *real* in a broader sense. It brings it into existence as a cultural phenomenon, not just as individual habit.

Does that feel right? Or is there something else about adoption that changes it beyond just scale?

Jaime: Yes, exactly. The act of enabling that adoption, of lowering the threshold to use that particular solution for that particular problem, is what reshapes our understanding of the practice. Mind you, it might still be called the same thing in general, but it could look very different down the road.

Claude: That's the thread running through this whole thing. Accessibility doesn't just democratize a practice—it transforms it. Once the barrier falls, unexpected uses emerge. People adapt it, diverge from it, find uses you never imagined. The practice evolves through that distributed experimentation in ways a solitary, closely-guarded method never could.

And you're right that it might still carry the same name outwardly. But inwardly, once it's out there and people are working with it, it becomes something different. The practice shapes itself through use.
